# Working Groups Reference

## The Initiative: Tokenizing the Financial System

GFTN's Tokenizing the Financial System Working Groups are a structured multi-stakeholder initiative translating foundational principles into actionable implementation pathways. The initiative spans the full tokenization stack — from digital money and payment rails to tokenised real-world assets, securities, funds, and capital markets infrastructure. Three working groups meet twice in person (Point Zero Forum + Insights Forum, both in 2026) and produce a consolidated report published at Singapore FinTech Festival in November 2026, in conjunction with BIS, IMF, Accion, and the Atlantic Council.

**Built on GFTN's 2025 Digital Assets programme** — including the Global Digital Assets Report (November 2025) and a closed-door Digital Money Roundtable convened with the GFTN International Advisory Board alongside central banks, international organisations, and technology innovators to establish foundational principles across regulatory, technology, and governance dimensions.

**The working groups sit beneath an Oversight Committee chaired by BIS and IMF** (Andréa Maechler, DGM at BIS; Tobias Adrian at IMF), comprising Deputy Governors from MAS (Leong Sing Chiong), Bank of England (Sarah Breeden), ECB (Piero Cipollone), and RBI (Rabi Sankar), plus other central banks and international organisations. This is the committee that reviews and shapes outputs. Private sector sponsors appear in the working groups — not the Oversight Committee — but their outputs go directly to this body for review. The prestige of the Oversight Committee is a key part of the pitch.

**Avoid saying the Oversight Committee "endorses" outputs** — GFTN is a neutral non-profit convener. Say they "review," "shape," or "guide" working group findings.

**The core problem** the initiative addresses: governance fragmentation has forced sub-optimal technical workarounds, and the actors who can solve problems at the right level are not sufficiently coordinated with those experiencing them. This shows up as: (1) misalignment across layers — technical workarounds for what are fundamentally governance problems; (2) regulatory ambiguity creating compliance drag; (3) unequal capacity and fragmented standards globally.

## Working Group 1: Interoperability

**Core question:** How do we build tokenised financial systems that work across ledgers, borders, currencies, and platforms — without fragmenting global markets or creating new infrastructure silos?

**Problem statements this WG addresses:**
- Cross-border digital payment interoperability (CBDC-to-CBDC, stablecoin-to-fiat, API standards)
- Technical and messaging standards (ISO 20022 adoption, data field harmonisation)
- Multi-CBDC arrangements and corridor-level pilots
- Private sector rail interoperability (payment network compatibility, atomic settlement)
- Tokenization legal frameworks — the governance and legal infrastructure for tokenised asset settlement across jurisdictions
- Cross-border supervisory coordination — how regulators in different jurisdictions coordinate oversight of cross-border digital money and tokenised asset flows
- Cross-layer governance misalignment — pain points being addressed at the application layer (smart contracts, token logic) that are fundamentally protocol or governance issues
- **Atomic Delivery vs Payment (DVP) for tokenised securities** — how tokenised bonds, fund units, or RWAs settle against tokenised cash across different ledgers without requiring a trusted intermediary; the foundational settlement question for tokenised capital markets
- **Cross-platform tokenised asset portability** — how an asset tokenised on one platform (e.g., Canton Network) can settle against a counterparty on a different platform (e.g., R3 Corda, public chain) without losing its legal, compliance, or settlement properties
- **Market infrastructure integration** — how tokenised assets connect to existing market infrastructure: CSDs (Euroclear, DTCC, Clearstream), CCPs, and custodians; the standards for interfacing tokenised markets with incumbent infrastructure are largely absent
- **Smart contract standardisation for financial instruments** — a tokenised bond or fund unit needs its smart contract to be legally and technically equivalent across jurisdictions; currently there are no shared standards, creating fragmentation at the instrument layer
- **Data and metadata standards for tokenised asset classes** — what information needs to travel with a tokenised bond vs. a fund unit vs. a commodity receipt vs. a payment is different; the absence of standards creates downstream compliance and interoperability failures

**Who fits WG1 — fit signals:**
- Core business involves moving money or assets across borders, ledgers, or platforms
- Commercial interest in how interoperability and settlement standards get set
- Operating multi-currency, cross-network, or cross-chain infrastructure
- Involved in payments network design, correspondent banking, CBDC infrastructure, or tokenised asset settlement
- Building tokenised RWA, securities, or fund infrastructure that needs to interoperate across counterparties

**Company archetypes that belong here:**
- Global payment networks (Visa, Mastercard, UnionPay, SWIFT)
- Cross-border fintech rails (Ripple, Wise/TransferWise, Banking Circle, Nium)
- Neo-banks with cross-border products (Revolut, Monzo, N26)
- Big Tech payments with global reach (GooglePay, ApplePay)
- Global banks with active tokenisation programmes (HSBC Orion, JPMorgan Onyx, Citi Token Services)
- CBDC infrastructure providers (G+D, Giesecke+Devrient, Idemia)
- Enterprise blockchain interoperability networks (Canton Network, R3 Corda)
- Capital markets infrastructure (Euroclear, DTCC, Clearstream, SIX Digital Exchange)
- Tokenisation platforms (Securitize, Broadridge)
- Crypto rail companies with RWA ambitions (Ripple/XRPL, Stellar)
- Regional payment systems (India's NPCI/UPI, PAPSS for Africa)

## Working Group 2: Regulation & Trust

**Core question:** How do we build a regulatory environment where tokenised financial assets — from stablecoins and CBDCs to tokenised securities, funds, and RWAs — are trusted by governments, institutions, and end users, without stifling innovation?

**Problem statements this WG addresses:**
- Regulatory harmonisation across jurisdictions for stablecoins, CBDCs, and digital assets
- Trust frameworks for digital currency issuers (reserve requirements, audit standards, disclosure)
- AML/CFT compliance in digital asset environments — including de-minimis thresholds, pre/post-transaction compliance obligations, and sanctions screening for stablecoins, DeFi, staking, and gas-fee liability
- Consumer and investor protection in digital money and tokenised asset markets
- Cross-border regulatory recognition and equivalence
- Conflating compliance governance with operational governance — a distinction frequently blurred in digital asset regulation
- **Securities regulation for tokenised RWAs** — when does a tokenised real-world asset become a security under MiFID II, SEC rules, or IOSCO frameworks? Currently resolved ad hoc by jurisdiction, creating compliance drag for any cross-border tokenisation programme
- **Regulatory frameworks for tokenised investment vehicles** — on-chain money market funds (BlackRock BUIDL, Franklin Templeton BENJI), tokenised ETFs, and tokenised private equity operate in a regulatory grey zone in most jurisdictions; existing fund regulation frameworks were not written for blockchain-native distribution
- **Custodian and safekeeping obligations for tokenised securities** — existing custody regulation assumes physical or book-entry securities; smart contract-based custody creates novel questions about liability, segregation, and what "holding" means
- **Prudential capital treatment of tokenised assets** — how Basel III/IV applies to bank holdings of tokenised securities and tokenised deposits is actively unresolved; affects every bank building tokenisation infrastructure
- **Legal enforceability of smart contracts as binding financial instruments** — whether a smart contract constitutes a binding financial agreement varies by jurisdiction; a prerequisite for institutional adoption at scale
- **Investor accreditation and distribution rules in tokenised markets** — tokenisation's inclusion promise depends on lowering minimum ticket sizes, but distribution rules in most jurisdictions restrict who can hold tokenised securities

**Who fits WG2 — fit signals:**
- Regulatory clarity is existential to their business model or growth strategy
- They issue, custody, distribute, or manage tokenised assets or digital currencies
- They operate in multiple jurisdictions and face fragmented regulatory regimes
- They want to be seen as the "compliant, responsible" player in their category
- Their business model depends on regulatory frameworks that don't yet exist (tokenised funds, tokenised securities, on-chain custody)

**Company archetypes that belong here:**
- Stablecoin issuers (Circle/USDC, Paxos/PYUSD)
- Crypto exchanges seeking legitimacy (Coinbase, Kraken)
- Digital asset custodians (Anchorage Digital, Fireblocks, BitGo, BNY Mellon Digital)
- Payment platforms with digital asset ambitions (PayPal, Stripe in stablecoin context)
- Compliance/RegTech firms (Chainalysis, Elliptic, TRM Labs)
- Asset managers with tokenised fund products (BlackRock, Franklin Templeton, Fidelity, WisdomTree)
- Traditional custodians expanding into digital assets (BNY Mellon, State Street, Northern Trust)
- Banks with tokenised deposit or settlement programmes (JPMorgan Onyx, Citi Token Services)
- Capital markets infrastructure with regulatory implications (SIX Digital Exchange)
- Sovereign wealth funds with digital asset portfolios (Temasek, GIC)

**Current WG2 roster — what's already there:**
- Strategic Contributors: 2 TBD (urgent gap)
- Active Contributors: Elise Soucie Watts (GDF), Nicole Sandler (Ubyx/CFIT) — both UK-based; 2 more TBD

- Legal/compliance practitioner (no law firm or in-house legal voice)
- Emerging market regulatory perspective (no Asian, African, or LatAm voice)
- Consumer/privacy rights perspective

## Working Group 3: Inclusion

**Core question:** How do we design tokenised financial systems that actively include the 1.4 billion unbanked and underbanked — and democratise access to asset classes previously available only to institutions and high-net-worth individuals?

**Problem statements this WG addresses:**
- Designing CBDCs and digital wallets for low-connectivity, feature-phone environments
- Closing the gender gap in digital financial access
- Cross-border remittances for low-income migrant workers
- SME and micro-enterprise access to digital payment infrastructure
- Digital identity as a prerequisite for financial inclusion
- Emerging economy access to stable savings via stablecoins
- Tokenization for SME financing and land titles
- Consumer education for first-time investors entering digital money and tokenised asset markets
- Safe on/off ramps between digital money systems and the cash economy
- **Fractional ownership of real-world assets for emerging market populations** — tokenisation can reduce minimum investment thresholds from $100,000 to $10, making real estate, commodities, and private credit accessible to populations currently locked out entirely; qualitatively different from the payments inclusion agenda
- **Tokenised bonds and fund units for retail micro-investors in emerging markets** — applying the BlackRock/Franklin Templeton tokenised fund model to emerging market sovereign bonds or local currency instruments to channel household savings into productive investment
- **Tokenised receivables for SME supply chain finance** — enabling SMEs locked out of traditional credit to tokenise invoices and receivables, unlocking working capital; the most commercially immediate inclusion application of tokenisation
- **Democratised access to private markets** — private equity and private credit restricted to institutional investors today; tokenisation can bring these to retail investors in emerging economies in ways that transform long-term wealth accumulation

**Who fits WG3 — fit signals:**
- Business model serves low-income, unbanked, or underserved populations
- Products operate in emerging markets (Sub-Saharan Africa, South and Southeast Asia, LatAm)
- Inclusion is a genuine commercial or mission differentiator, not just an ESG footnote
- Working on fractional asset ownership, tokenised SME finance, or democratised investment access
- Technology choices (mobile-first, offline-capable, low-cost) designed for excluded populations

**Company archetypes that belong here:**
- Mobile money operators (Safaricom/M-Pesa, MTN Mobile Money, Airtel Money)
- Blockchain foundations with inclusion mission (Stellar, Celo, Interledger, Hyperledger)
- Tech companies with emerging market product lines (Amazon — sellers, Google — India/SEA)
- Open source financial infrastructure (Linux Foundation via Hyperledger)
- CBDC vendors in low-income countries (G+D)
- Remittances platforms (WorldRemit/Sendwave, Ria Money Transfer)
- Women's financial inclusion specialists (Women's World Banking)
- SME-focused digital finance (Ant Group/Alipay, M-Kopa)
- Tokenised RWA platforms targeting emerging market retail access (Ondo Finance, Centrifuge)
- Asset managers with emerging market retail tokenisation programmes

**Current WG3 roster — what's already there:**
- Strategic Contributors: FATF (TBD name), CGAP (TBD name)
- Active Contributors: IFC-SMEFF, Asian Development Bank, UNCDF, IMF, Lisa Tan (Economics of Token Engineering), CDPI, AFI, FSB, World SME Forum, Consumers International, Better than Cash Alliance
- Participants: Amazon (Kennie or Sujit), Stellar Development Foundation, Interledger Foundation, Hyperledger/Linux Foundation, ConsenSys Software, Celo Foundation, G+D, Ant Group
- Report Development Leads: CCAF (Yogesh), Accion CFI (Jael)

**What perspectives are still missing in WG3:**
- Mobile money operator (M-Pesa/Safaricom — most critical missing voice)
- Remittances practitioner
- Tokenised RWA platform with emerging market retail focus (Ondo Finance, Centrifuge)
- Women's financial inclusion voice
- A government/finance ministry from a leading digital inclusion country (Kenya, Bangladesh, Tanzania)
- Asset manager with emerging market retail tokenisation product

## Cross-WG Fit Notes

Some companies fit multiple WGs and may be pitched for more than one:
- **Stripe**: WG1 (payment rail interoperability) + WG2 (stablecoin regulatory harmonisation)
- **HSBC**: WG1 (Orion tokenised asset settlement + CBDC pilots) — clear primary
- **Canton Network**: WG1 (enterprise tokenised asset interoperability) — clear primary
- **G+D**: WG1 (CBDC infrastructure) + WG3 (inclusion-focused deployments)
- **Ripple**: WG1 (XRPL payment and tokenised asset settlement) + WG2 (regulatory legitimacy)
- **Coinbase**: WG1 (crypto-native interoperability) + WG2 (regulatory legitimacy)
- **Temasek**: WG2 (regulatory trust for portfolio) — primary; WG1 secondary
- **BlackRock**: WG2 (tokenised fund regulatory frameworks) primary; WG1 secondary (BUIDL cross-chain interoperability)
- **Franklin Templeton**: WG2 (tokenised fund regulation pioneer) primary; WG1 secondary
- **Stellar**: WG1 (cross-border settlement) + WG3 (inclusion mission)

When pitching a company for two WGs, position it as a choice — let them pick the one most aligned with their current strategic priorities — rather than asking for a double commitment upfront.

## New Company Profiles: Asset Managers & Capital Markets

### BlackRock
**Primary WG:** WG2
**Sponsorship angle:** BlackRock's BUIDL fund ($500M+ in tokenised US Treasuries) is the largest proof of concept for on-chain institutional investment vehicles — but the regulatory framework that lets it scale globally doesn't yet exist. WG2 is where the fund regulation, custodian obligations, and investor distribution rules for tokenised investment vehicles get written. Larry Fink has publicly called tokenisation the next evolution of markets; WG2 is where that evolution gets its rules.
**Pitch:** *"BUIDL proved the concept. WG2 is where the regulatory framework that lets it scale gets written — before others define it for you."*
**Target individuals:**
- **Senior contact:** Robbie Mitchnick (Head of Digital Assets) or Chief Investment Officer
- **Working-level contact:** Head of Digital Asset Policy or Chief Legal Officer (digital assets division)

---

### Franklin Templeton
**Primary WG:** WG2
**Sponsorship angle:** Franklin Templeton's BENJI fund put a US-registered investment fund on a public blockchain first. They understand better than any other asset manager what regulatory ambiguity in tokenised fund distribution looks like in practice. WG2 is where those ambiguities get resolved — and Franklin Templeton should be the firm that helps resolve them, not the one that has to adapt to a framework written by others.
**Pitch:** *"Franklin Templeton pioneered the tokenised fund. WG2 is where the industry framework follows — be the firm that shaped it."*
**Target individuals:**
- **Senior contact:** Jenny Johnson (CEO) or Roger Bayston (Head of Digital Assets)
- **Working-level contact:** Head of Digital Asset Policy or Head of Blockchain/Digital Assets

---

### Euroclear
**Primary WG:** WG1
**Sponsorship angle:** Euroclear is the incumbent European CSD — the infrastructure layer that tokenised securities settlement must interface with to achieve institutional adoption at scale. WG1's interoperability frameworks for tokenised assets are meaningless without Euroclear's participation; they're the bridge between the tokenised world and the existing market infrastructure that holds $37 trillion in assets.
**Pitch:** *"Tokenised asset interoperability frameworks that don't account for CSD integration aren't interoperable. Euroclear's participation in WG1 is what makes the standard real."*
**Target individuals:**
- **Senior contact:** CEO or Chief Digital Officer
- **Working-level contact:** Head of Digital Securities or Head of Innovation

### Securitize

They are the infrastructure layer for institutional RWA tokenisation and have more real-world implementation data on what tokenised asset interoperability requires than almost any other participant.
**Pitch:** *"Securitize is where institutional tokenisation actually happens. WG1 needs that implementation evidence — not just the theory."*

- **Senior contact:** CEO Carlos Domingo

### BNY Mellon
**Primary WG:** WG2
**Sponsorship angle:** BNY Mellon is the world's largest custodian ($46 trillion in assets under custody) and is actively building digital asset custody capabilities. The custodian and safekeeping regulatory frameworks for tokenised securities being developed in WG2 directly define what BNY Mellon's digital custody business must look like — and they have the most to gain from a framework that reflects operational reality rather than theoretical models.
**Pitch:** *"BNY Mellon custodies $46 trillion. The rules for custodying tokenised versions of those assets are being written now — be in the room."*
**Target individuals:**
- **Senior contact:** CEO Robin Vince or Chief Digital Officer
- **Working-level contact:** Head of Digital Assets or Head of Regulatory Affairs (digital)

### SIX Digital Exchange (SDX)

Their experience with FINMA, their integration with the Swiss National Bank's wholesale CBDC, and their live tokenised securities market makes them the most credible voice in WG2 on what functional regulation for tokenised capital markets actually looks like.
**Pitch:** *"SDX already operates under the regulatory framework WG2 is trying to design. That experience is the most valuable input in the room."*
